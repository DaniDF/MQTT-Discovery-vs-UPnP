\begin{abstract}
This paper provides a comparative evaluation of Universal Plug and Play (UPnP) and Message Queuing Telemetry Transport-Discovery (MQTT-D) based on two fundamental performance metrics: latency and robustness. The analysis examines the behavior of both protocols under similar deployment conditions, with particular attention to dense network environments, where numerous devices simultaneously attempt to discover one another. The experimental findings indicate that UPnP consistently surpasses MQTT-D in both aspects, demonstrating lower latency and higher robustness across all tests. These results suggest that UPnP may represent a more effective solution for dense application scenarios in which rapid response times are essential.
\end{abstract}

\IEEEPARstart{T}{he} concept of \gls{iot} has been described through a variety of definitions in the literature. The International Telecommunication Union is \textit{"A global infrastructure for the information society, enabling advanced services by interconnecting (physical and virtual) things based on existing and evolving interoperable information and communication technologies"}\cite{ITU_Y.2060}. In a complementary manner, IEEE identifies a set of essential characteristics that distinguish \gls{iot}. Specifically, it is viewed as a networked infrastructure in which objects are uniquely identifiable, communicate through the Internet in at least part of their communication process, and are capable of sensing, actuating, or both. Furthermore, such objects are expected to be programmable through the integration of intelligence, while the services they provide should be accessible at any time, in any place, and for any entity.

In a environment populated by a large variety of different devices with different needs and capabilities designing a standard useful for communicating with them is a challenge.
Discovery protocols are designed to reconcile the dichotomy between the requirement for highly specific, ad-hoc protocols capable of granular device control and the necessity for generalised frameworks that can accommodate a highly heterogeneous ecosystem of devices.
By employing these protocols, an \gls{iot} device achieves autonomous configuration through active participation in the advertisement and solicitation of network-based services.
Devices functioning as service providers systematically broadcast their available resources, the specific services they offer, and the operational guidelines for invocation, such as the appropriate \gls{api}. Furthermore, discovery protocols streamline the connectivity paradigm between clients and target resources or services. 
Consequently, this architecture ensures that clients are adequately equipped to transmit necessary input parameters to the services and successfully retrieve the corresponding computational results.

In particular this dissertation is focused on \gls{upnp} and a protocol based on \gls{mqtt} knwon as \gls{mqttd}. \gls{upnp} is a discovery protocol without infrastructural nodes where each node can advertise and listen for services. In particular nodes that make available services are called \textit{devices} and those searching for services are called \textit{control-points}.
In \gls{mqttd} there is a third entity called \textit{broker} that facilitates the communication between services and clients.

This dissertation wants to highlight the performance differences between \gls{upnp} and \gls{mqttd} in \gls{iot} environment. The main focus is measuring and comparing latency and robustness in different deployment configurations: how different amount of data producers and consumers affect the protocol performances.
Section \ref{sec_discovery_protocols_for_iot} provides an overview of \gls{iot} protocol categorisation, followed by a detailed description of the two protocols under consideration. Section \ref{sec_iot_discovery_protocols_analysis} outlines the requirements for the experimental evaluation and describes the methodology adopted for conducting the tests. Section \ref{sec_experimental_results} presents and discusses the obtained results, while Section \ref{sec_conclusions} summarises the main findings and concludes this dissertation.

\section{Discovery Protocols for IoT}
\label{sec_discovery_protocols_for_iot}
% * Definizione di discovery per IoT
% * Descrivere i protocolli (con immagini)
% * Descrizione del problema

This section discuss about \gls{iot} discovery protocols with particular focus on {\gls{mqttd}} and \gls{upnp}.

\gls{iot} environments are characterise by a large variety of different devices therefore there must be mechanisms available for automatic discovery of resources, their properties and capabilities as well as the means to access them \cite{Resource_discovery_in_IoT}.
In \cite{Categorization_Discovery_IoT} are presented different categorizations for \gls{iot} discovery technologies, this dissertation will be focused only on \textit{"Searching on My Network"}. A further distinction can be made based on the topology that these protocols implement, in particular they can be categorised into: centralised and distributed.
In centralised approaches, resources are registered in a directory that serves as intermediary between providers and users. In distributed approaches, users discover active resources through direct communication with resources in a \gls{p2p} fashion with no need for an intermediary directory \cite{comp_ana_disc}.

\gls{upnp} is a \gls{p2p} distributed discovery service without hierarchical structuring.
In \gls{upnp} two entities can be distinguished: \textit{control-point} the entity that look for services and resources and \textit{device} the entity that make services available.
A \textit{device} can dynamically join a network and obtain a locally valid \textit{IP} address. After it can make its capabilities visible and discover others, if present, and dynamically leave the network.
\gls{upnp} uses \textit{XML} for the description of the resources, services and action invocations. 

\begin{figure}[!ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{results/upnp_protocol_stack.png}
    \caption{UPnP Protocol Stack \cite{ocf_upnp_architecture_2020}}
    \label{fig:upnp_prtocol_stack}
\end{figure}

The \gls{upnp} protocol stack is based on four parts: \gls{ssdp} \cite{ocf_upnp_architecture_2020}, \gls{http} \cite{TimBLee_W3}, \gls{soap} \cite{ocf_upnp_architecture_2020} and \gls{gena} \cite{ocf_upnp_architecture_2020}.
\gls{ssdp} is a network protocol based on the Internet protocol suite for advertisement and discovery of network services and presence information. It is a text-based protocol based on a non fully compliant version of \gls{http} over UDP \cite{ocf_upnp_architecture_2020}.

In \gls{ssdp}, whenever a node intends to discover a \textit{device}, it issues an $\text{M-SEARCH}$ request specifying the desired device characteristics. Upon receiving this request, a \textit{device} determines whether it satisfies the specified criteria and, if so, returns an appropriate response. Since the number of potential responses cannot be known in advance, a burst of simultaneous replies on the network may lead to overloading of the \textit{control point}. To mitigate this risk, the \textit{control point} includes the $\text{MX}$ parameter in the search request \cite{ocf_upnp_architecture_2020}. This parameter defines the maximum number of seconds within which responding \textit{devices} may transmit their replies. Consequently, each \textit{device} waits for a random interval between 0 and the value specified by $\text{MX}$ before sending its response.

\gls{soap} is a messaging protocol specification for exchanging structured information in XML message format. It is used by the \textit{control-point} for: (i) requesting the execution of services, \gls{rpc}, with specified arguments, (ii) on the \textit{device} and by the \textit{device} for returning the results.
\gls{gena} is an event notification protocol used by \textit{devices} to publish variables updates to subscribed \textit{control-points}. \gls{gena} supports both unicast and multicast event notification.

\gls{mqttd} is a centralised discovery protocol \gls{mqtt}-based \cite{taxonomy_IoT}. \gls{mqtt} is a lightweight publish-subscribe messaging protocol designed for constrained devices. \gls{mqttd} is a metadata‑exchange convention built on top of the \gls{mqtt} protocol that standardises how devices and services describe themselves and publish their configuration to an \gls{mqtt} broker. It enables automatic detection, registration, and integration of devices in home‑automation or IoT ecosystems without requiring manual configuration.

The \gls{mqtt} protocol assigns a specific \gls{qos} level to each message, establishing the delivery guarantees required across potentially unreliable networks. The protocol defines three hierarchical levels of service: \gls{qos} 0 (At-Most-Once), which provides best-effort delivery without retransmission mechanisms; \gls{qos} 1 (At-Least-Once), which ensures delivery but permits the possibility of duplicate messages; and \gls{qos} 2 (Exactly-Once), the most complex level, which employs a multi-step handshake to guarantee that a message is delivered once and only once. Furthermore, publishers can append a \textit{retained} flag to a message. This instructs the \gls{mqtt} broker to persist the last message for a given topic, ensuring that any newly subscribed clients immediately receive the most recent data upon connection, independent of the initial publication event.

As \gls{mqttd} does not define a standardised role-based classification for clients, this dissertation adopts, for the sake of terminological clarity, the same naming convention used in \gls{upnp}. Accordingly, entities that provide services are referred to as \textit{devices}, whereas entities that discover and make use of such services are referred to as \textit{control-points}.

\section{\gls{iot} Discovery Protocols Analysis}
\label{sec_iot_discovery_protocols_analysis}
This dissertation aims to evaluate the performance of two of the main \gls{iot} discovery protocols, \gls{mqttd} and \gls{upnp}. Such an evaluation is particularly relevant in dense \gls{iot} scenarios, including smart factory floors, smart cities, and extensive agricultural sensor grids, where system reliability and rapid device discovery are critical to maintaining overall network performance. For this reason the evaluated metrics for this study are latency and robustness. The first wants to measure the time needed for the execution of the first interaction between \textit{control-point} and \textit{device} after the discovery phase. The second wants to measure the ratio between the discovered devices and the actual deployed devices.

\subsection{Implementation}
% * Descrivere il fatto di aver implementato UPnP/MQTT
% * Mettere descrizione dei componenti software dei protocolli
% * Differenze rispetto allo standard (MX non limitato a 5)
% * Motivare la scelta delle tecnologie

To establish a testing environment, a fully compatible \gls{upnp} \textit{device} and \textit{control point} were implemented in accordance with the official specification \cite{ocf_upnp_architecture_2020}. All major protocol components were developed, including \gls{ssdp}, \gls{soap}, and \gls{gena} \cite{DaniDF_MQTT-Discovery_vs_UPnP}. For \gls{http}, the standard library provided by Go was employed. To further validate standards compliance and interoperability, a third-party \gls{upnp} client library was also used \cite{goupnp}.

For \gls{mqttd}, an existing implementation of the \gls{mqtt} protocol was adopted, while only a wrapper for the \gls{mqttd} data format was developed. As the \gls{mqtt} broker, Mosquitto was selected due to its widespread adoption and popularity in IoT deployments.

\gls{mqttd} defines two mechanisms for \textit{device} presence discovery. The first relies on a retained message published by a \textit{device} when it joins the network; this message is subsequently delivered by the broker to a \textit{control point} when the latter subscribes to the corresponding topic \cite{mqttd}. The second mechanism is based on solicitation: the \textit{control-point} publishes a message on a predetermined topic to which all \textit{devices} are subscribed. Upon receiving such a solicitation message, each \textit{device} responds by sending its discovery message\cite{mqttd}.

The software developed for this work \cite{DaniDF_MQTT-Discovery_vs_UPnP} supports both mechanisms; however, the experiments were conducted exclusively using the solicitation-based approach.
All software components developed by the author \cite{DaniDF_MQTT-Discovery_vs_UPnP} were implemented in Go in order to balance high execution efficiency with the advantages of a higher-level programming language.

\section{Experimental Results}
\label{sec_experimental_results}
% * Sintesi obiettivo degli esperimenti
% * Come fatto setup
% * Foto setup sperimentale
This section evaluates latency and robustness in a two-node \gls{upnp} deployment, in which one node runs the \textit{devices} and the other runs the \textit{control-points}. The \gls{mqttd} deployment requires the addition of a third node, which hosts the \gls{mqtt} broker. Each node is equipped with 12 core CPU, 16GB of memory and 2.5Gb lan interface.

Experimental evaluations were executed in a closed, dedicated network infrastructure to isolate the testing equipment. All experiments were performed over both wired and wireless (IEEE 802.11n \cite{ieee_802.11n}) connections. The deployment of \textit{devices} and \textit{control-points} was scaled incrementally: initially in unit steps from 1 to 10, and subsequently in decadal steps up to 100. For \gls{mqtt}, each configuration was evaluated across \gls{qos} levels 0, 1, and 2. To mitigate statistical variance, each experimental combination was repeated 30 times, and the results were averaged.

The experiments tried to evaluate the effectiveness of the $\text{MX}$ parameter in the \gls{upnp} protocol by removing the random wait time from the \textit{device} response.
The following subsections present the measured metrics for configurations of \textit{devices} and \textit{control-points} of value 1, 5, 10, 50, 100 but a more detailed version can be found in appendix \ref{apx_more_data_points}.

Since in \gls{upnp} the \gls{rpc} mechanism is implemented through \gls{soap} that has a client-server approach, it is also interesting a comparison between \gls{gena} and \gls{mqttd} both publisher-subscriber.

In particular, the single-\textit{device} deployment exhibits an error rate close to 0\%, and each subsequent increase in deployed \textit{devices} leads to a gradual degradation in performance across the different \textit{control-point} deployments. This behaviour contrasts with that observed in the wireless scenario, in which all deployed \textit{devices} exhibit a more uniform trend across configurations as the number of \textit{control points} increases.

A comparison between the wireless and wired \gls{mqttd} configurations does not reveal any significant improvement in either latency or robustness. The experimental results are nearly identical for both metrics across the two scenarios.

Based on the evaluations conducted in this dissertation, \gls{upnp} achieves the lowest latency, whereas \gls{mqttd} provides the highest level of robustness. These results suggest that \gls{upnp} is more suitable for \gls{iot} networks with a limited number of \textit{devices}, such as smart homes or small-scale facilities. In contrast, \gls{mqttd}, due to its greater robustness, appears more appropriate for dense \gls{iot} environments, in which a large number of \textit{devices} must perform discovery and exchange data concurrently.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec_conclusions}
% * Cosa è stato fatto in breve
% * Cosa è stato dimostrato
% * Future directions
This dissertation undertook a comparative evaluation of \gls{mqttd} and \gls{upnp} in dense network environments, with particular focus on latency and robustness under varying deployment configurations of \textit{devices} and \textit{control-points}. The experimental scenarios were assessed within a real network infrastructure specifically established for the purposes of this study. Each configuration was tested in both wired and wireless environments, the latter employing IEEE 802.11n technology.

For the purposes of these experiments, a fully compliant \gls{upnp} \textit{device} and \textit{control-point} were implemented. Nevertheless, to ensure compliance with the specifications, part of the \textit{control-point} functionality relied on a third-party library.

The experimental results show that \gls{upnp} achieves the lowest latency across all evaluated configurations, making it more suitable for small-scale \gls{iot} networks. In contrast, \gls{mqttd} demonstrates greater robustness across all adopted \gls{qos} levels, which makes it more appropriate for denser \gls{iot} deployments.

A comparison of wired and wireless deployments shows that \gls{upnp} benefits significantly from the wired configuration, with lower latency and greater robustness, whereas performance in the wireless case degrades more rapidly as the deployment scales. In contrast, \gls{mqttd} exhibits nearly identical latency and robustness in both scenarios. Therefore, the connection type has a significant impact on \gls{upnp} but a negligible effect on \gls{mqttd}.

Future research could investigate how \gls{upnp} and \gls{mqttd} behave with real scenario data, investigating how payloads affect latency and robustness. In addition a further study could repeat this experiments in smart homes or industrial plant networks and evaluate how performances degrades in a shared network.
