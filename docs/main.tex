\gls{iot} has a lot of definitions: from International Telecommunication Union is \textit{"A global infrastructure for the information society, enabling advanced services by interconnecting (physical and virtual) things based on existing and evolving interoperable information and communication technologies"}. IEEE defines \gls{iot} as a list of features:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A network
    \item Objects are identifiable as things with unique identifiers
    \item When a thing communicate should use internet at least in one segment of the communication
    \item Things can have sensing or actuating or both capabilities
    \item They should be programmable as result of injection of intelligence
    \item The offered services should be available anywhere, anytime and for anything
\end{itemize}

In a environment populated by a large variety of different devices with different needs and capabilities designing a standard useful for communicating with them is a challenge.
Discovery protocols are designed to reconcile the dichotomy between the requirement for highly specific, ad-hoc protocols capable of granular device control and the necessity for generalised frameworks that can accommodate a highly heterogeneous ecosystem of devices.
By employing these protocols, an \gls{iot} device achieves autonomous configuration through active participation in the advertisement and solicitation of network-based services.
Devices functioning as service providers systematically broadcast their available resources, the specific services they offer, and the operational guidelines for invocation, such as the appropriate \gls{api}. Furthermore, discovery protocols streamline the connectivity paradigm between clients and target resources or services. 
Consequently, this architecture ensures that clients are adequately equipped to transmit necessary input parameters to the services and successfully retrieve the corresponding computational results.

In particular this dissertation is focused on \gls{upnp} and a protocol based on \gls{mqtt} knwon as \gls{mqttd}. \gls{upnp} is a discovery protocol without infrastructural nodes where each node can advertise and listen for services. In particular nodes that make available services are called \textit{devices} and those searching for services are called \textit{control-points}.
In \gls{mqttd} there is a third entity called \textit{broker} that facilitates the communication between services and clients.

This dissertation wants to highlight the performance differences between \gls{upnp} and \gls{mqttd} in \gls{iot} environment. The main focus is measuring and comparing latency and robustness in different deployment configurations: how different amount of data producers and consumers affect the protocol performances.

\section{Discovery protocols for IoT}
% * Definizione di discovery per IoT
% * Descrivere i protocolli (con immagini)
% * Descrizione del problema

Resource discovery protocols can be categorised into: centralised and distributed. In centralised approaches such as \gls{mqtt}, resources are registered in a directory that serves as intermediary between providers and users. In distributed approaches such as \gls{upnp}, users discover active resources through direct communication with resources in a \gls{p2p} fashion with no need for an intermediary directory \cite{comp_ana_disc}.

\gls{upnp} is a \gls{p2p} discovery service without hierarchical structuring.
In \gls{upnp} two entities can be distinguished: \textit{control-point} the entity that look for services and resources and \textit{device} the entity that make services available.
A device can dynamically join a network and obtain a locally valid \textit{IP} address. After it can make its capabilities visible and discover others, if present, and dynamically leave the network.
\gls{upnp} uses \textit{XML} for the description of the resources, services and action invocations. 

\begin{figure}[!ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{results/upnp_protocol_stack.png}
    \caption{UPnP Protocol Stack \cite{ocf_upnp_architecture_2020}}
    \label{fig:upnp_prtocol_stack}
\end{figure}

\gls{upnp} can be divided into 4 parts: \gls{ssdp}, \gls{http}, \gls{soap} and \gls{gena}.
\gls{ssdp} is a network protocol based on the Internet protocol suite for advertisement and discovery of network services and presence information. It is a text-based protocol based on a non fully compliant version of \gls{http} over UDP \cite{ocf_upnp_architecture_2020}.

In \gls{ssdp}, whenever a node intends to discover a \textit{device}, it issues an $\text{M-SEARCH}$ request specifying the desired device characteristics. Upon receiving this request, a \textit{device} determines whether it satisfies the specified criteria and, if so, returns an appropriate response. Since the number of potential responses cannot be known in advance, a burst of simultaneous replies on the network may lead to overloading of the \textit{control point}. To mitigate this risk, the \textit{control point} includes the $\text{MX}$ parameter in the search request \cite{ocf_upnp_architecture_2020}. This parameter defines the maximum number of seconds within which responding \textit{devices} may transmit their replies. Consequently, each \textit{device} waits for a random interval between 0 and the value specified by $\text{MX}$ before sending its response.

\gls{soap} is a messaging protocol specification for exchanging structured information in XML message format. It is used by the \textit{control-point} for requesting the execution of services with specified arguments on the \textit{device} and by the \textit{device} for returning the results.
\gls{gena} is an event notification protocol used by \textit{devices} to publish variables updates to subscribed \textit{control-points}. \gls{gena} supports both unicast and multicast event notification.

\gls{mqttd} is a discovery protocol based on \gls{mqtt}. \gls{mqtt} is a lightweight publish-subscribe messaging protocol designed for constrained devices. \gls{mqttd} is a metadata‑exchange convention built on top of the \gls{mqtt} protocol that standardises how devices and services describe themselves and publish their configuration to an \gls{mqtt} broker. It enables automatic detection, registration, and integration of devices in home‑automation or IoT ecosystems without requiring manual configuration.

The \gls{mqtt} protocol assigns a specific \gls{qos} level to each message, establishing the delivery guarantees required across potentially unreliable networks. The protocol defines three hierarchical levels of service: \gls{qos} 0 (At-Most-Once), which provides best-effort delivery without retransmission mechanisms; \gls{qos} 1 (At-Least-Once), which ensures delivery but permits the possibility of duplicate messages; and \gls{qos} 2 (Exactly-Once), the most complex level, which employs a multi-step handshake to guarantee that a message is delivered once and only once. Furthermore, publishers can append a \textit{retained} flag to a message. This instructs the \gls{mqtt} broker to persist the last message for a given topic, ensuring that any newly subscribed clients immediately receive the most recent data upon connection, independent of the initial publication event.

This dissertation aims to evaluate the performance of \gls{mqttd} and \gls{upnp} in deployments with different number of deployed devices and different number of control devices. For \gls{mqttd} will be evaluated how the performances changes with respect to the requested \gls{qos}.
The evaluated metrics for this study are latency and robustness. The first wants to measure the time needed for discovery and the execution of the first interaction between \textit{control-point} and \textit{device}. The second wants to measure the ratio between the discovered devices and the actual deployed devices. \textcolor{blue}{\hl{TODO Da vedere magari da girare perché i grafici mostrano l'errore quindi la percentuale dei mancanti non dei presenti}}

\section{Implementation}
% * Descrivere il fatto di aver implementato UPnP/MQTT
% * Mettere descrizione dei componenti software dei protocolli
% * Differenze rispetto allo standard (MX non limitato a 5)
% * Motivare la scelta delle tecnologie

To establish a testing environment, a fully compatible \gls{upnp} \textit{device} and \textit{control point} were implemented in accordance with the official specification \cite{ocf_upnp_architecture_2020}. All major protocol components were developed, including \gls{ssdp}, \gls{soap}, and \gls{gena} \cite{DaniDF_MQTT-Discovery_vs_UPnP}. For \gls{http}, the standard library provided by Go was employed. To further validate standards compliance and interoperability, a third-party \gls{upnp} client library was also used \cite{goupnp}.

For \gls{mqttd}, an existing implementation of the \gls{mqtt} protocol was adopted, while only a wrapper for the \gls{mqttd} data format was developed. As the \gls{mqtt} broker, Mosquitto was selected due to its widespread adoption and popularity in IoT deployments.

\gls{mqttd} defines two mechanisms for \textit{device} presence discovery. The first relies on a retained message published by a \textit{device} when it joins the network; this message is subsequently delivered by the broker to a \textit{control point} when the latter subscribes to the corresponding topic \cite{mqttd}. The second mechanism is based on solicitation: the \textit{control-point} publishes a message on a predetermined topic to which all \textit{devices} are subscribed. Upon receiving such a solicitation message, each \textit{device} responds by sending its discovery message\cite{mqttd}.

The software developed for this work \cite{DaniDF_MQTT-Discovery_vs_UPnP} supports both mechanisms; however, the experiments were conducted exclusively using the solicitation-based approach.

All software components developed by the author \cite{DaniDF_MQTT-Discovery_vs_UPnP} were implemented in Go in order to balance high execution efficiency with the advantages of a higher-level programming language.

\section{Experimental results}
% * Sintesi obiettivo degli esperimenti
% * Come fatto setup
% * Foto setup sperimentale

Experimental evaluations were executed in a closed, dedicated network infrastructure to isolate the testing equipment. All trials were performed over both wired and wireless (IEEE 802.11n) connections. The deployment of \textit{devices} and \textit{control-points} was scaled incrementally: initially in unit steps from 1 to 10, and subsequently in decadal steps up to 100. For \gls{mqtt}, each configuration was evaluated across \gls{qos} levels 0, 1, and 2. To mitigate statistical variance, each experimental combination was repeated 30 times, and the results were averaged.

The experiments tried to evaluate the effectiveness of the $\text{MX}$ parameter by removing the random wait time from the \textit{device} response.

The following subsections present the measured metrics for values 1, 5, 10, 50, 100 but a more detailed version can be found in appendix \ref{apx_more_data_points}.

\subsection{Latency comparison}
% * Espandere obiettivo test (motivazione)
% * Descrizione esperimento (metodi)

\begin{figure}[!ht]
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{results/upnp_latency_light.png}
    \label{fig_upnp_latency_light}
    \caption{\gls{upnp} latency comparison with different \textit{devices} and \textit{control-points}. See \ref{fig_apx_upnp_latency}.}
    \label{fig_upnp_latency}
\end{figure}

The comparison shown in Figure \ref{fig_upnp_latency} reveals a clearly distinguishable performance difference between the configuration with a single deployed \textit{device} and those with higher numbers of deployed \textit{devices}. Furthermore, Figure \ref{fig_apx_upnp_latency_wireless} in Appendix \ref{apx_more_data_points} shows that the difference between the configurations with 2 and 3 deployed devices further supports the observation that setups with fewer deployed \textit{devices} are associated with higher latency. By contrast, configurations involving more than 10 devices yield relatively stable latency values, which begin to diverge noticeably only when the number of \textit{control points} exceeds 30.

\begin{figure}[!ht]
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{results/mqtt_latency_light.png}
    \caption{\gls{mqtt} latency comparison with different \textit{devices} \textit{control-points} and \gls{qos}. See \ref{fig_apx_mqtt_latency_wireless}.}
    \label{fig_mqtt_latency}
\end{figure}

The latency measurements obtained from the \gls{mqttd} tests indicate a clear distinction between the lower \gls{qos} levels, \gls{qos} 0 and 1, and the highest level, \gls{qos} 2. As shown in Figure \ref{fig_mqtt_latency}, configurations with the minimum number of deployed \textit{control-points} exhibit a latency of approximately 600\,ms. This value rises to around 1200\,ms in the case of 2 deployed \textit{control-points}, after which a sharp decrease is observed. By contrast, this behaviour is not evident in Figure \ref{fig_mqtt_latency}, where the latency values corresponding to 1 and 2 deployed \textit{control-points} remain similar, and the subsequent reduction does not occur at the same configuration point. Instead, elevated latency values persist up to at least 5 deployed \textit{control points}.

\subsubsection{Comparing the results}
The comparison between Figure \ref{fig_upnp_latency} and Figure \ref{fig_mqtt_latency} indicates that \gls{upnp} consistently exhibits lower latency than \gls{mqttd}. For configurations with more than one deployed \textit{control point}, the observed latency values are approximately eight times lower than those of the best-performing \gls{mqttd} configuration.

\subsection{Robustness comparison}
\begin{figure}[!ht]
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{results/upnp_robustness_light.png}
    \label{fig_upnp_robustness_light}
    \caption{\gls{upnp} robustness comparison with different \textit{devices} and \textit{control-points}. See \ref{fig_apx_upnp_robustness}.}
    \label{fig_upnp_robustness}
\end{figure}

For \gls{upnp}, the ratio between the number of \textit{devices} discovered by the \textit{control points} and the actual number of deployed \textit{devices} is observed to be consistent across the different configurations (Figure \ref{fig_upnp_robustness}). The error rate increases rapidly, following an approximately logarithmic trend up to 20 deployed \textit{control points}; it then remains almost constant until 40 \textit{control points}, after which it rises sharply toward 100\%. A more detailed representation in Figure \ref{fig_apx_upnp_robustness} further confirms that this behaviour is nearly identical for all considered configurations.

\begin{figure}[!ht]
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{results/mqtt_robustness_light.png}
    \caption{\gls{mqtt} robustness comparison with different \textit{devices} \textit{control-points} and \gls{qos}. See \ref{fig_apx_mqtt_robustness_wireless}.}
    \label{fig_mqtt_robustness}
\end{figure}

The results obtained for \gls{mqttd} show that the error rate rises sharply from almost 0\% to 100\% when the number of deployed \textit{control-points} approaches 10 for every configuration. This suggests that, during the 10-second interval reserved for the discovery phase, none of the \textit{control points} was able to receive the discovery announcement and respond within the required time frame.

\subsubsection{Comparing the results}
A comparison of the robustness of the two discovery protocols indicates that \gls{mqttd} exhibits greater robustness when only a very small number of \textit{control points} is deployed. However, once the threshold of 10 deployed \textit{control points} is reached, \gls{mqttd} displays an error rate of 100\%, whereas \gls{upnp} still maintains a lower, although substantial, error rate corresponding to approximately 60\% of \textit{devices} not being discovered.
